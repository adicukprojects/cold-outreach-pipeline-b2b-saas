I've worked at 10 companies, and performance reviews have ranged from mildly useful to an utter circus (when there was one at all).

Part of the problem is frequency:

When reviews happen once a year, people mostly remember the last project you worked on.

When they happen every week, the feedback gets very tactical, and there is not enough time to actually apply it and improve.

Part of it is also quality:

How much can you really coach someone if you don't have clear examples of what happened and when? Or if you don't have enough data to identify clear patterns?

Part of it is the structure:

"So, how do you think you are doing?" vs a 10-page form you need to fill out in advance.

As a founder, I used to keep one Google Doc per direct report and add notes whenever I noticed something during the week.

That kind of worked... But man, that became way too much admin, way too quickly. And good luck re-reading all the notes from past sessions.

So I just stopped doing it.

That gave me a lot of time back and mental space, but it also meant a lot of growth opportunities were missed for my direct reports.

Fortunately, now that I'm working on a business operating system, I can change that. We already use the system as a place for business metrics, so it makes sense to add individual performance data points as well.

I'm curious to know how people are handling this these days?

Do you have a system for capturing ongoing feedback, or is it still mostly memory plus last-minute review prep? Or did you also just give up?
