Poet Louise Glück once wrote:

"We look at the world once, in childhood. The rest is memory."

Storytime. When I was a kid, my dad would take me to the hardware store on Saturday mornings.

I don't remember what we bought.

I don't remember the conversations.

But I remember the feeling.

The smell of lumber.

The hum of fluorescent lights.

The giant wall of screws and nails that felt as fascinating as a museum.

Back then, nothing was ordinary.

A hardware store wasn't a hardware store.

It was an adventure.

Somewhere along the way, familiarity replaced wonder.

The things we see every day become invisible.

The street we drive down.

The person we eat dinner with.

The dog waiting at the door.

The body that carries us around.

The ordinary Tuesday afternoon.

We stop seeing them and start remembering them.

Maybe that's why loss hurts so much.

Not because something disappears.

Because for a brief moment, we're forced to see it again.

As if for the first time.

The other day, I found myself watching a butterfly drift across my path.

Ten-year-old Josh would've stopped everything to follow it.

Fifty-seven-year-old Josh almost kept walking.

Maybe that's the invitation hidden in Glück's quote:

To look at what we've grown used to as though we've just arrived.

The coffee.

The conversation.

The walk.

The butterfly.

The world hasn't become less interesting.

We've just gotten used to it.
